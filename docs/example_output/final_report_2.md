# Evolution of AI Models for Raw Sequence Data in Computational Biology: A 2019–2025 Comparative Landscape

## Executive Summary
- **Transformer-based architectures have become the dominant model class for processing DNA, RNA, and protein sequences, driven by their ability to capture long-range dependencies, scale efficiently to massive datasets, and support zero-/few-shot transfer learning ([Nature Methods](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41592-024-02523-z), [MDPI Biology](https://mdpi-res.com/d_attachment/biology/biology-12-01033/article_deploy/biology-12-01033.pdf)).**
- **Adaptive tokenization strategies—ranging from fixed k-mer approaches to learned, biology-informed G-grams and dynamic tokenization methods—have been developed to address the complexity of ultra-long genomic sequences, improving both accuracy and data efficiency ([MxDNA](https://export.arxiv.org/pdf/2306.15794v1.pdf), [DNAZEN](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2505.02206)).**
- **Multi-task and cross-modal transfer learning frameworks, exemplified by models such as Omni-DNA, Life-Code, and IsoFormer, yield significant performance gains by leveraging shared biological priors and enabling robust predictions across diverse tasks including structure prediction, function annotation, and variant effect prediction ([Omni-DNA](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.03499v1), [IsoFormer](https://openreview.net/pdf?id=xImeJtdUiw)).**
- **Real-world deployments in clinical, industrial, and academic settings have demonstrated that integrated design models, retrieval-augmented protein encoders, and privacy-preserving frameworks are now bridging the gap between laboratory benchmarks and translational applications ([AlphaGenome](https://storage.googleapis.com/deepmind-media/papers/alphagenome.pdf), [OncoKB](https://www.nature.com/articles/s43018-025-00991-6)).**
- **Emerging standardized benchmark suites and regulatory-ready evaluation protocols are becoming critical to guide further model development while addressing data bias, scalability, and interpretability challenges in raw-sequence AI systems ([GenBench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.01627), [DART-Eval](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.05430)).**

## Dominant Architectures and Model Evolution

Between 2019 and 2025, the field of AI for computational biology has witnessed a decisive shift from traditional recurrent or convolutional architectures to transformer-based models. Transformers have rapidly emerged as the dominant model class due to their ability to capture long-range dependencies inherent in genomic data, parallelize computations efficiently, and support expansive unsupervised pretraining schemes. Early work such as DNABERT (2021) demonstrated that treating DNA as natural language through fixed k-mer tokenization and masked language modeling could yield robust representations for tasks like promoter prediction and splice-site identification ([DNABERT](https://academic.oup.com/bioinformatics/article/37/15/2112/6128680)). Subsequent developments like DNABERT-2 further improved efficiency with BPE tokenization and introduced multi-species benchmarks such as Genome Understanding Evaluation (GUE). Reviews and surveys conducted between 2023 and 2025 corroborate that transformer-based genomic language models (gLMs) consistently outperform RNNs and CNNs in both accuracy and scalability, particularly under data-scarce conditions and in cross-species generalization scenarios ([Nature Machine Intelligence](https://www.nature.com/articles/s42256-025-01007-9), [Frontiers in Genetics](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/genetics/articles/10.3389/fgene.2025.1634882/full)).

The evolution of these models has been driven not solely by incremental improvements in architecture but also by strategic innovations that target the unique challenges of genomic data. For instance, hybrid architectures that integrate transformer attention with state-space models (e.g., HyenaDNA) have enabled scaling to ultra-long sequence lengths while preserving single-nucleotide detail, and adaptive tokenization methods have significantly enhanced performance on regulatory tasks. This rapid evolution is closely tied to the growing availability of diverse, large-scale genomic datasets and the increasing need to deploy models in real-world clinical and industrial settings.

## Adaptive Tokenization for Ultra-Long Sequences

Adaptive tokenization techniques have emerged as a critical innovation in processing ultra-long DNA, RNA, and protein sequences. Traditional fixed k-mer tokenization, although effective in some contexts, often fails to capture the discontinuities, overlaps, and biological ambiguities native to genomic sequences. Recent developments such as LDARNet, MxDNA, and MergeDNA introduce adaptive tokenization schemes that learn token boundaries in a data-driven manner. LDARNet, for example, utilizes a hierarchical encoder-decoder configuration to dynamically adjust token boundaries and improve histone modification prediction, thereby generating compressed latent representations that preserve key biological signals ([LDARNet](https://openreview.net/pdf/31831cb766186c8ed093e89abbb27266e1352ffd.pdf)).

Similarly, MxDNA implements a sparse Mixture of Convolution Experts with deformable convolutions, outperforming fixed-tokenization baselines with less pretraining data and reducing computational demand while maintaining accuracy ([MxDNA](https://export.arxiv.org/pdf/2306.15794v1.pdf)). Adaptive tokenization not only fosters enhanced efficiency and accuracy but also facilitates long-context modeling, as seen in HyenaDNA’s approach that eliminates tokenization concerns by preserving single-nucleotide fidelity over contexts as long as 1,000,000 tokens ([HyenaDNA](https://paperswithcode.com/paper/hyenadna-long-range-genomic-sequence-modeling)). Moreover, dual-tokenization frameworks like BiRNA-BERT blend nucleotide-level resolution with adaptive methods, achieving state-of-the-art performance in RNA-specific tasks while still enabling long-range predictions ([BiRNA-BERT](https://www.nature.com/articles/s42003-025-08982-0)).

These innovations underscore an important trend: adaptive, biologically informed tokenization strategies are essential for capturing the complex regulatory grammar and structural nuances of genomic sequences. They also highlight an emerging research direction that calls for standardized evaluation frameworks and interpretability tools to map learned tokens back to biologically meaningful units such as motifs or promoters.

## Advances in Bioinformatics Tasks

The main bioinformatics tasks tackled by raw sequence AI models over the last six years include protein structure prediction, function annotation, variant effect prediction, and de novo protein design. The advent of AlphaFold2 revolutionized protein structure prediction by achieving near-experimental accuracy with its end-to-end deep learning approach, and subsequent models like AlphaFold3 and AlphaFold Multimer have extended these advances to multi-chain protein complexes ([AlphaFold2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41580-019-0163-x), [AlphaFold Multimer](https://www.nature.com/articles/s42003-025-08960-6)). These models have been benchmarked extensively using standardized datasets such as CASP and ProteinNet, which have historically driven improvements in model design and evaluation.

Meanwhile, protein function annotation and variant effect prediction have benefited greatly from the emergence of protein language models (PLMs) and retrieval-augmented approaches. Early PLMs generated embeddings that improved function prediction benchmarks, while advanced models such as PoET-2 have demonstrated superior zero-shot predictive performance on mutations and indels, all the while operating under low-data settings with reduced computational overhead ([PoET-2](https://openreview.net/pdf/adc4910b537c69dd20edb81f4706191564f08caa.pdf)). Additionally, integrated design frameworks combining sequence, structure, and function—exemplified by ESM3—aim to co-design proteins with novel folds by jointly modeling the underlying fitness landscape. These integrated approaches are beginning to reach parity with and even surpass modular, task-specific pipelines, showing promise for accelerating the protein design cycle in drug discovery and enzyme engineering ([ESM3](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.ads0018)).

Furthermore, tasks that require multi-chain assembly predictions, such as protein–protein interaction modeling and complex docking, have seen improvements through models like AlphaFold-Multimer and deep graph neural network-based docking scores. These advancements underscore the increasing capacity of raw sequence AI models to address higher-order structural challenges that are pivotal for real-world applications in drug discovery, despite remaining gaps in reliably modeling dynamic and heterogeneous interactions.

## Complex Assemblies and Multi-Chain Interactions

Understanding dynamic, multi-chain protein assemblies is crucial for numerous biomedical applications, especially drug discovery. AlphaFold-Multimer remains a strong baseline for predicting inter-chain interactions; however, its performance can fluctuate with complex heteromeric ensembles, necessitating interface-specific metrics like pDockQ2 to accurately assess quality ([AlphaFold-Multimer](https://scispace.com/papers/evaluation-of-alphafold-multimer-prediction-on-multi-chain-2aknx3yo)). Benchmark studies using docking scoring functions from deep learning frameworks (DeepRank-GNN, dMaSIF) have shown substantial improvements over classical methods, confirming the critical role of deep learning in assessing molecular interactions ([DeepRank-GNN](https://bmcbioinformatics.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12859-024-05991-4)).

Meanwhile, benchmarks such as PoseBench have emerged to provide comprehensive evaluations for protein–ligand docking in complex multi-chain contexts, revealing that while deep learning-based co-folding methods generally outperform traditional docking, they can still underperform in novel binding poses or multi-ligand scenarios ([PoseBench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.14108)). In addition, specialized metrics like the DCI score and robust ensemble strategies have been deployed to gauge interface accuracy, particularly where global structural similarity metrics like TM-score are insufficient on their own.

These findings illustrate that while transformer-based and hybrid architectures have achieved remarkable accuracy in isolated tasks, multi-chain and complex assembly predictions demand integrated evaluation frameworks and innovative scoring regimes to capture subtle inter-chain dynamics and conformational variability.

## Cross-Modal Transfer Learning Strategies

Cross-modal transfer learning has emerged as a powerful paradigm to harness the interconnected nature of biological sequences, spanning DNA, RNA, and proteins. Approaches such as IsoFormer have demonstrated that a carefully designed multi-modal framework—employing encoder-specific pretraining coupled with a fusion stage—can achieve state-of-the-art results in isoform expression prediction across tissues while also enabling efficient data transfer across modalities ([IsoFormer](https://openreview.net/pdf?id=xImeJtdUiw)). This is further bolstered by models like Life-Code, which employs a codon tokenizer to jointly process DNA, RNA, and protein sequences through a unified long-sequence encoder, thereby capitalizing on the central dogma of molecular biology ([Life-Code](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.07299v1)).

Other frameworks, such as ORCA and OmniBioTE, have further advanced the field by demonstrating that selective freezing of modality-specific encoders and focused fine-tuning on the fusion layers can lead to substantial gains in data efficiency. These studies reveal that full transfer learning and adapter-based fine-tuning yield better cross-modal representations compared to naive, full fine-tuning strategies, particularly when auxiliary tasks are included to preserve biological interpretability ([ORCA](https://export.arxiv.org/pdf/2302.05738v2.pdf), [OmniBioTE](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.16245)).

The promise of cross-modal approaches lies not only in improving predictive performance on traditional tasks (e.g., isoform-level expression, binding energy prediction) but also in enabling models to generalize across modalities with minimal labeled data—essential for applications where one data type (e.g., clinical RNA-seq) is abundant while another (e.g., protein structure) is scarce. This research underlines the importance of developing standardized cross-modal evaluation platforms to precisely measure the impact of embedding alignment quality and encoder fine-tuning strategies on downstream performance.

## Data Efficiency and Transferability in Multi-Omics Models

Data efficiency has become a central concern as genomic and proteomic datasets continue to grow both in size and complexity. Multi-task and transfer learning frameworks such as Omni-DNA and Life-Code have demonstrated that pretraining on vast amounts of raw sequence data, followed by multi-task fine-tuning, can significantly reduce per-task data requirements while simultaneously improving model generalizability. Omni-DNA, for instance, leverages a two-stage training process that first pretrains on DNA sequences and then fine-tunes using multi-task objectives to achieve state-of-the-art results on 18 out of 26 tasks spanning acetylation, methylation, and other functional outputs ([Omni-DNA](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.03499v1)).

Moreover, models like IsoFormer show that cross-modal transfer learning further enhances data efficiency. By capitalizing on pretrained representation from related modalities, these models can achieve robust performance on low-data tasks such as differential isoform expression prediction, outperforming single-modality baselines even when labeled data are limited ([IsoFormer](https://openreview.net/pdf?id=xImeJtdUiw)). Multitask approaches have been particularly effective in settings like drug discovery, where models like MAMMAL—which was trained on over 2 billion samples—demonstrated superior performance relative to their single-task counterparts by sharing signal across genomics and proteomics tasks ([MAMMAL](https://arxiv.org/html/2410.22367v1)).

Together, these findings indicate that data efficiency gains largely arise from the sharing of representations across tasks and modalities, resulting in models that are not only faster and less resource-intensive to fine-tune but also exhibit increased robustness across diverse conditions. However, a challenge remains in establishing unified evaluation protocols that can directly quantify these gains across disparate tasks and datasets.

## Encoding Strategies for Raw Sequence Data

The choice of encoding strategy is central to the performance, scalability, and interpretability of raw sequence AI models. Recent studies have moved away from traditional one-hot and fixed k-mer embeddings towards more advanced techniques that incorporate biological priors. HyenaDNA, for example, uses single-nucleotide tokens coupled with long-range implicit convolution to maintain nucleotide-level resolution over extraordinary context lengths, enabling single-nucleotide precision even in ultra-long sequences ([HyenaDNA](https://paperswithcode.com/paper/hyenadna-long-range-genomic-sequence-modeling)).

Innovations such as DNAZEN have introduced multi-granular tokenization through G-grams, which are generated via pointwise mutual information (PMI) to capture both local and long-range dependencies. DNAZEN’s approach has shown significant improvements on promoter, transcription factor binding, and splice site tasks compared to fixed k-mer tokenizers ([DNAZEN](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2505.02206)). Other models like the Genomic Tokenizer (GT) propose biology-informed tokenization that aligns with natural genomic segmentation (e.g., codons, start/stop signals), directly incorporating central dogma knowledge into the encoding process ([GT](https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2025.04.02.646836v1.full.pdf)).

These evolving encoding strategies empower models to better balance the trade-off between granularity and computational cost. Multi-granular tokenizers not only capture complex motifs but also enable adaptive tokenization that is particularly valuable for ultra-long sequence contexts, where fixed-token approaches often fall short. Optimization of tokenization strategies remains an active research area, with emerging leads pointing to the benefits of hybrid approaches that combine the strengths of fixed k-mer and adaptive, biology-informed techniques.

## Gaps, Future Challenges, and Standardized Benchmarks

Despite significant advances, several gaps and challenges remain in the domain of raw sequence AI models. One major bottleneck is the lack of universally accepted benchmark datasets and standardized evaluation protocols that cover the full spectrum of genomic tasks, especially tasks involving low-homology sequences and long-range interactions. Current benchmarks such as ProteinNet, CASP-series datasets, and the emerging GenBench and DART-Eval suites address parts of the problem but often lack consistency across task domains and species ([GenBench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.01627), [DART-Eval](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.05430)).

Interpretability is another significant challenge. Many state-of-the-art models, particularly transformer-based architectures, operate as black boxes with limited insight into how learned embeddings correlate to biological features such as motifs or structural domains. There is an urgent need to develop visualization tools and interpretability frameworks that can map token boundaries back to known biological phenomena, thereby increasing trust and actionable insights for practitioners.

Scalability and compute efficiency also continue to pose challenges. While techniques such as sub-quadratic attention (e.g., HyenaDNA) and context compression modules (e.g., FOCUS) have made strides in efficiently processing ultra-long sequences, balancing resource demands with accuracy remains a trade-off that requires further refinement. Moreover, addressing bias and data heterogeneity—particularly when transitioning models from laboratory research to clinical and industrial applications—demands careful attention to dataset provenance and standardized cross-task benchmarking.

Future research should focus on:
- Developing unified benchmark suites that cover a diverse range of genomic tasks with standardized, cross-task evaluation protocols.
- Investing in interpretability tools that can elucidate the biological significance of learned tokens and model decisions.
- Exploring architectural innovations that further reduce computational overhead while preserving accuracy in ultra-long and multi-modal settings.
- Establishing regulatory-ready evaluation criteria and data-citation practices to support future clinical and industrial deployments.

## Integrated Sequence–Structure–Function Co-Design in Protein Engineering

A particularly promising area is the integrated sequence–structure–function co-design paradigm for protein engineering. Models such as ESM3, PoET-2, and RFdiffusion have demonstrated the capacity to integrate multiple aspects of protein biology into a single end-to-end design process. ESM3, in particular, has shown that co-design frameworks can generate functional proteins with high divergence from known proteins, capturing evolutionarily distant fitness landscapes and enabling rapid design cycles ([ESM3](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.ads0018)).

By integrating structural and functional considerations directly into the design process, these models reduce reliance on separate, task-specific pipelines and enable faster iterations in laboratory settings. End-to-end co-design has been shown to improve efficiency in protein design challenges, such as generating high-quality enzyme backbones and novel protein folds in fewer iterative cycles compared to traditional methods. This integration not only expedites the design process but also enhances predictive accuracy in real-world design scenarios where multiple constraints must be satisfied simultaneously.

Further research should concentrate on quantifying the advantages of integrated co-design frameworks over modular approaches across real-world design tasks, exploring metrics like cycle time reduction, sequence divergence, and experimental validation success rates. Such studies will provide a more granular understanding of the trade-offs and benefits inherent in joint modeling strategies.

## Key Datasets Shaping the Field

The progress in raw sequence AI has been underpinned by the development of several critical datasets and benchmarks that provide standardized training and evaluation environments. ProteinNet, established in 2019, set a precedent by offering standardized splits and high-quality MSAs, and it anchored much of the progress observed in the AlphaFold era ([ProteinNet](https://bmcbioinformatics.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12859-019-2932-0)). Subsequent benchmark initiatives—including the CASP series, AlphaFold Protein Structure Database, FoldBench, and PoseBench—have continuously refined the evaluation framework for protein structure prediction, variant effect prediction, and docking tasks.

Recent efforts have focused on addressing long-range genomic interactions and regulatory tasks, with benchmarks like DNALONGBENCH and LRB specifically assessing model performance on tasks up to 1 million base pairs. These datasets illustrate that while large language models achieve strong performance on many conventional tasks, challenges remain in tasks that demand extensive context and unbiased evaluation in regions with low sequence homology ([DNALONGBENCH](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-025-65077-4), [LRB](https://openreview.net/pdf/052b920235f117a049dc43e5647fa3585040c71b.pdf)).

In parallel, multi-modal benchmarks that link genomics with proteomics—such as GENO-PROT—are emerging, underscoring the necessity of cross-domain benchmarks that capture the integrative, multi-omics challenges faced in real-world applications. Continued efforts in this area are essential to ensure that progress in model development translates into clinically and industrially relevant improvements.

## Long-Sequence Efficient Architectures

Scaling models to process full-genome sequences while maintaining single-nucleotide resolution poses extraordinary challenges due to inherent computational constraints. Innovations such as Life-Code, Evo, HybriDNA, and HyenaDNA represent significant breakthroughs in this area. Life-Code, for instance, unifies multi-omics inputs by leveraging a codon-aware tokenizer and a hybrid transformer architecture, thereby enabling efficient processing of ultra-long sequences with structured biological priors ([Life-Code](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.07299v1)). 

Evo and HybriDNA further demonstrate that models with billions of parameters can operate with context lengths extending to tens or even hundreds of kilobases, while novel architectural approaches like sub-quadratic attention models dramatically reduce computation relative to standard transformers. HyenaDNA, by preserving single-nucleotide resolution over sequences up to 1 million tokens and achieving up to 160× faster training than traditional dense transformers, has set an impressive benchmark for long-sequence efficiency ([HyenaDNA](https://paperswithcode.com/paper/hyenadna-long-range-genomic-sequence-modeling)).

Context compression methods such as those implemented in FOCUS have also shown promise, reducing memory demands by summarizing long sequences without significant loss of accuracy. Although these approaches continue to face trade-offs between granularity and computational efficiency, they underscore a promising path forward for full-genome modeling in both research and clinical contexts.

## Model Evolution and Structural Drivers

A critical analysis of structural drivers—the “three whys”—reveals why transformers have become the architecture of choice for genomic sequence modeling. First, transformers adeptly capture the long-range dependencies vital for understanding regulatory interactions and chromatin architecture, addressing the limitations of RNNs and CNNs that suffer from fixed-context or vanishing gradient problems ([Nucleotide Transformer](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41592-024-02523-z)). Second, the parallelizable nature of transformers enables efficient processing of extremely large genomic datasets, a necessity in an era defined by big data in biology. Third, the flexible attention mechanisms inherent to transformers facilitate unsupervised pretraining and zero-/few-shot transfer learning, thereby improving performance on diverse downstream tasks ranging from structure prediction to variant effect estimation ([HyenaDNA](https://paperswithcode.com/paper/hyenadna-long-range-genomic-sequence-modeling)).

These drivers not only justify the widespread adoption of transformer-based approaches but also inspire continuing innovation toward hybrid architectures that integrate sub-quadratic attention and adaptive tokenization techniques to mitigate computational overhead while maximizing predictive performance.

## Multitask vs. Single-Task Fine-Tuning and Transfer Learning Effects

Multi-task and transfer learning have become central to maximizing model utility in computational biology. Research has shown that multitask fine-tuning leads to performance gains by leveraging shared biological signals across related tasks. Omni-DNA, for example, has demonstrated that multitask fine-tuning outperforms single-task approaches in predicting acetylation/methylation profiles by unifying information across multiple genomic benchmarks ([Omni-DNA](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.03499v1)). 

Similarly, models like IsoFormer utilize cross-modal transfer learning, where pretrained encoders across DNA, RNA, and protein modalities are combined to enhance prediction accuracy for tissue-specific isoform expression. Early research (e.g., UniRep and the PEER benchmark) supported the premise that embeddings derived from unsupervised pretraining on massive protein sequence datasets are highly transferable across multiple downstream tasks, reducing the reliance on large, task-specific labeled datasets ([UniRep](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41592-019-0598-1), [PEER Benchmark](https://papers.nips.cc/paper/2022/file/e467582d42d9c13fa9603df16f31de6d-Paper-Datasets_and_Benchmarks.pdf)).

The overall trend indicates that leveraging shared representations across tasks not only improves generalization but also allows for more efficient use of limited labeled data, a crucial factor for applications in clinical diagnostics and drug discovery. Future work in this area should focus on systematic benchmarking of multitask versus single-task paradigms and cross-modal transfer strategies to further delineate optimal training approaches.

## Privacy-Preserving Deployment Frameworks

As raw sequence AI models transition from research to clinical and industrial deployment, preserving data privacy and meeting regulatory requirements have become paramount. Recent frameworks such as COLLAGENE, OmicSHIELD, PP-GWAS, SECRET-GWAS, and sPLINK demonstrate that incorporating techniques like differential privacy (DP), homomorphic encryption (HE), secure multi-party computation (MPC), and federated learning can enable secure, scalable, and compliant analyses on sensitive genomic data ([COLLAGENE](https://genomebiology.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s13059-023-03039-z), [OmicSHIELD](https://journals.plos.org/ploscompbiol/article?id=10.1371/journal.pcbi.1012626)).

These solutions are designed to keep raw data localized while enabling collaborative model training and inference in cloud environments. Clinical deployments, such as those demonstrated with DELPHI-2M and Clinical Transformer models, have started to integrate these privacy-preserving methodologies with state-of-the-art prediction models, achieving regulatory-grade performance at population scale ([Clinical Transformer](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-025-57181-2)). Moreover, robust explainability pipelines are being integrated within these frameworks to ensure that model decisions can be audited and verified, which is essential for clinical trust and regulatory approval.

The challenge going forward is to balance the trade-offs between computational efficiency, data privacy, and interpretability while ensuring that cloud-based solutions can sustain real-time performance at scale.

## Retrieval-Augmented Protein Encoders

Retrieval-augmented approaches represent another promising direction by integrating external sequence information—such as homologous sequences or exemplars—into protein encoding workflows. Models like Profluent-E1 and RSA have shown that conditioning protein encoders on retrieved sequences significantly enhances zero-shot prediction accuracy in tasks like fitness prediction and contact-map estimation ([Profluent-E1](https://storage.googleapis.com/e1-paper-a26c3c79/profluent-e1.pdf), [RSA](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2302.12563)). This strategy reduces reliance on extensive MSA-based preprocessing and allows for rapid inference, attributes that are invaluable in industrial and clinical contexts where labeled data are sparse.

Furthermore, retrieval-augmented pipelines, including those in AIDO.RAGFold, demonstrate substantial speed gains compared to models like AlphaFold2 while maintaining comparable accuracy in structure prediction ([AIDO.RAGFold](https://www.mlsb.io/papers_2024/Retrieval_Augmented_Protein_Language_Models_for_Protein_Structure_Prediction.pdf)). These methods also underscore the potential for hierarchical MSA retrieval and fine-tuned exemplars to create efficient, nonparametric augmentations that substantially improve data efficiency and generalization.

Future efforts should focus on piloting these retrieval-augmented models against industry-standard benchmarks and systematically comparing their performance on low-data and rapid-deployment tasks.

## Real-World Deployments in Academic, Clinical, and Industrial Settings

The transition from bench to practice is evident in several high-profile real-world deployments of raw sequence AI models. In academic settings, large-scale datasets such as the AlphaFold Protein Structure Database have revolutionized structural biology by providing millions of predicted protein structures that are used for hypothesis generation and drug target discovery ([AlphaFold Protein Structure Database](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41597-024-03403-z)). 

In clinical applications, autonomous AI agents have been developed for oncology decision-making and rare disease diagnosis, achieving high diagnostic accuracy and expert-level agreement on clinical cases ([OncoKB](https://www.nature.com/articles/s43018-025-00991-6), [DeepRare](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-10097-9)). Models that integrate multiple data modalities—from electronic health records and histopathology images to genomic sequencings such as cfRNA—are now being used to predict disease trajectories and treatment responses ([Delphi-2M](https://doi.org/10.1101/2024.06.07.24308553), [HEX](https://www.npg.nature.com/articles/s41591-025-04060-4)).

Industrial deployments have also begun leveraging models like AlphaGenome, which predicts thousands of genomic tracks across multi-modal datasets and has demonstrated state-of-the-art performance on a variety of functional tasks ([AlphaGenome](https://storage.googleapis.com/deepmind-media/papers/alphagenome.pdf)). These practical applications underscore that the innovations developed in raw sequence AI research are beginning to address real-world challenges, although issues related to data interoperability, privacy, and interpretability remain key considerations for widespread adoption.

## Standardized Benchmarks and Regulatory-Ready Protocols

A critical requirement for future progress is the development of standardized benchmark suites that can reliably evaluate raw sequence AI models across diverse tasks and domains. Recent initiatives such as GenBench, GeneBench, DART-Eval, DNALONGBENCH, and the LRB (Long-Range Benchmark) have established robust frameworks for assessing models on tasks ranging from regulatory element prediction to long-range structural modeling ([GenBench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.01627), [DART-Eval](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.05430)). These benchmarks incorporate low-homology filtering and bias-robust metrics to ensure fairness and reproducibility while providing regulatory-ready evidence that supports clinical and industrial claims.

Standardizing evaluation protocols is essential to reduce the fragmentation seen across studies, enable direct comparisons across model classes, and promote reproducibility in scientific research. Future work must include the development of formal protocols that address data provenance, bias audits, and pre-registration of evaluation pipelines to facilitate regulatory acceptance.

## Structural Drivers: The “Three Whys” of Transformer Adoption

Understanding why transformer-based models have so decisively overtaken older architectures involves a layered “three whys” analysis. First, transformers excel at modeling long-range dependencies—a critical requirement for capturing interactions between distant genomic elements such as enhancers, promoters, and silencers. Second, the inherent parallelism in transformer architectures allows for significantly faster training and scaling properties compared to sequential models like RNNs. Third, transformers support flexible representation learning via attention mechanisms, enabling robust zero- and few-shot transfer learning and providing superior performance on diverse downstream tasks. Evidence from foundational papers on the Nucleotide Transformer and HyenaDNA supports these claims, demonstrating that transformers not only improve predictive performance but also enable practical scaling to ultra-long sequence contexts ([Nucleotide Transformer](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41592-024-02523-z), [HyenaDNA](https://paperswithcode.com/paper/hyenadna-long-range-genomic-sequence-modeling)). 

This analysis validates the widespread industry and academic shift towards transformer-based models, while also highlighting the ongoing need for architectural innovation to further address scalability and efficiency issues.

## Transfer Learning Effects and Multitask Training

Transfer learning has consistently been shown to enhance the generalizability of models across various biological tasks. Early works like UniRep demonstrated that self-supervised pretraining on millions of protein sequences yields embeddings that are transferable to downstream tasks such as stability and function prediction ([UniRep](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41592-019-0598-1.pdf)). Modern benchmarks like PEER reinforce the observation that multitask fine-tuning generally offers superior results compared to single-task approaches and often leads to a reduction in the required labeled data per task ([PEER Benchmark](https://papers.nips.cc/paper/2022/file/e467582d42d9c13fa9603df16f31de6d-Paper-Datasets_and_Benchmarks.pdf)).

Models like ProtST further illustrate that integrating auxiliary modalities (text-based biomedical descriptions, imaging, etc.) with protein sequence data can lead to enhanced zero-shot performance and more robust functional predictions ([ProtST](https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.12040)). The combined evidence suggests that further exploration of transfer and multitask learning frameworks, especially across the central dogma, holds significant promise for increasing both data efficiency and practical utility.

## Conclusions and Recommendations

The evolution of AI models for raw sequence data in computational biology from 2019 to 2025 reveals a clear trajectory driven by the need to capture long-range genomic dependencies, improve computational scalability, and integrate multi-omics data for robust prediction and design. Transformer-based architectures have become the workhorse of the field, bolstered by adaptive tokenization techniques that enable modeling of ultra-long sequences with single-nucleotide resolution. Multi-task and cross-modal transfer learning strategies further enhance model performance and data efficiency, while privacy-preserving frameworks and standardized benchmarks provide the foundation for real-world deployment in clinical and industrial settings.

Key recommendations for future research include:
- **Developing unified benchmark suites and standardized evaluation protocols** to facilitate direct comparisons across model classes and ensure regulatory readiness, particularly through initiatives that incorporate low-homology filtering and bias audits.
- **Investigating adaptive tokenization methods and multi-granular encoding strategies** that preserve biological priors and ensure interpretability, including the creation of visualization tools for mapping learned tokens to biological motifs.
- **Expanding research into multi-task and cross-modal transfer learning** frameworks that can further leverage shared knowledge across DNA, RNA, and protein tasks, thereby reducing data requirements and supporting applications in drug discovery and clinical diagnostics.
- **Exploring hybrid and sub-quadratic attention architectures** (e.g., HyenaDNA, FOCUS) to push the boundaries of long-sequence modeling to full-genome scales while optimizing computational efficiency.
- **Implementing and rigorously evaluating privacy-preserving and federated learning frameworks** to address the pressing data-sharing and regulatory challenges that currently impede the clinical translation of genomic AI models.
- **Quantifying the integrated design advantages in protein co-design paradigms**, thereby enabling further optimization of end-to-end pipelines that combine sequence, structure, and function prediction for rapid and cost-effective protein engineering.

Overall, the rapid evolution of raw sequence AI models, driven by transformer architectures, adaptive tokenization, and advanced transfer learning techniques, has opened new horizons for computational biology. As these methodologies continue to mature and integrate with standardized benchmarks and privacy-preserving frameworks, we can expect significant advancements in the prediction, design, and therapeutic application of biological sequences across diverse real-world settings.
