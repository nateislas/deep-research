# Comprehensive Assessment of Spatial Reasoning in World Models: Capabilities, Failure Modes, and Gaps

## Executive Summary
- **World models show promising spatial reasoning capabilities when trained with 2D synthetic supervision augmented by 3D ground‐truth signals, yet limitations persist in long-horizon planning and viewpoint invariance** ([SPAR-7M/SPAR-Bench](https://openreview.net/pdf/6621fdc2d197d605ddbc0aaaea1e93b868df1d37.pdf), [SpatialThinker](https://arxiv.org/html/2511.07403)).
- **Object-centric tracking architectures that integrate depth signals and view-invariant latent representations improve cross-view and temporal consistency, though challenges remain under occlusion and dynamic scenes** ([SAVi++](https://openreview.net/pdf?id=fT9W53lLxNS), [SIMONe](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2106.03849)).
- **Unified and scalable benchmark suites are emerging to evaluate world models on multi-view, multi-modal 3D spatial reasoning tasks, yet standardization and cross-dataset generalization require further refinement** ([Ego3D-Bench](https://arxiv.org/html/2509.06266v2), [3DSRBench](https://www.cs.jhu.edu/~alanlab/Pubs25/ma20253DSRBench.pdf)).
- **Explicit 3D representations (point clouds, voxel/TSDF, and hybrid implicit methods) offer distinct trade-offs in memory efficiency, scalability, and planning accuracy, underscoring the need for geometry-aware evaluation metrics** ([PointWorld](https://openreview.net/pdf/820606bc13c8aca1c4e185ad46e51f278ae71741.pdf), [GRWM](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.26782)).
- **Memory fusion and robust per-slot temporal coherence techniques emerge as critical enablers for long-horizon spatial understanding across dynamic contexts, though further work is needed to mitigate latent drift and support robust reasoning under noise and occlusion** ([3DLLM-MEM](https://openreview.net/pdf/8e3474ff3b680185f73a397352845c1347b77d73.pdf), [SOLD](https://openreview.net/pdf/96985506cc6e6b51174eaf942ed996801b5fccbd.pdf)).

## Introduction
World models are increasingly vital in robotics, autonomous navigation, AR/VR, and embodied AI systems where understanding spatial layouts, dynamic object trajectories, and multi-view scene integration are essential. Researchers have advanced methods that explicitly incorporate 3D signals into training regimens, leverage object-centric representations, and develop unified benchmark suites to assess spatial reasoning. This report synthesizes a large body of evidence demonstrating advances in training signals, representation fidelity, temporal coherence, and generalization, as well as uncovering critical failure modes that remain unaddressed. Our primary objective is to assess world models’ capacity to reason over spatial data and articulate the key gaps that must be closed to enable robust, reliable spatial cognition in real-world tasks.

## Representing Space and Geometry in World Models
A fundamental challenge in world modeling lies in accurately representing space and geometry. Modern approaches integrate explicit 3D representations such as point clouds, voxel grids, and implicit neural fields to fuse perceptual information with geometric priors. For instance, the 3DLLM-MEM approach couples a memory-enhanced embodied agent with a dynamic 3D memory module that stores spatial–temporal features, enabling efficient retrieval and fusion of geometric information when planning in multi-room environments ([3DLLM-MEM](https://openreview.net/pdf/8e3474ff3b680185f73a397352845c1347b77d73.pdf)). Similarly, GRWM enforces geometric resemblance among latent representations along sensory trajectories and demonstrates that introducing explicit geometry priors markedly improves long-horizon rollout fidelity ([GRWM](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.26782)). 

Persistent storage structures, whether using point clouds, TSDFs, or hybrid implicit-explicit solutions, serve as anchors that ground the perception of object boundaries, spatial relations, and depth. Models such as PointWorld predict full-scene 3D point flow from RGB-D inputs and actions, thereby enabling zero-shot simulation in real-world manipulation tasks ([PointWorld](https://openreview.net/pdf/820606bc13c8aca1c4e185ad46e51f278ae71741.pdf)). In augmenting 2D training signals with lifted 3D annotations, SPAR-7M and its companion SPAR-Bench have demonstrated that leveraging 3D ground-truth–derived labels facilitates strong spatial relation reasoning with the scalability offered by 2D synthetic supervision ([SPAR-7M/SPAR-Bench](https://openreview.net/pdf/6621fdc2d197d605ddbc0aaaea1e93b868df1d37.pdf)). 

The development of unified 3D memory structures, including volumetric maps and hierarchical representations, underscores a trend away from flat, 2D-only representations toward architectures that encode persistent spatial geometry. Models like the 4D Latent World Model and Persistent Embodied World Model accumulate discretized 3D maps from video inputs that support planning over extended horizons ([4D Latent World Model](https://openreview.net/pdf/6006283ebc60cdb5adba8a2d57ddbc1644e5c5aa.pdf), [Persistent Embodied World Model](https://openreview.net/pdf/3e6af07877577010580a978ae5fa896dc94a397c.pdf)). Together, these approaches lay the foundation for robust spatial reasoning by coupling perceptual input with geometric memory systems that persist and update over time.

## Tracking, Multi-View Reasoning, and Depth Integration
Accurate tracking of objects over time and across different viewpoints forms the backbone of spatial reasoning in dynamic environments. Object-centric models achieve this by learning to extract view-invariant latent representations that encapsulate object identity separately from view-dependent features. For example, SIMONe employs a dual-latent strategy to isolate object-centric content from momentary frame-specific elements, delivering demonstrated success in achieving stable multi-view tracking and view synthesis ([SIMONe](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2106.03849)). 

Slot Structured World Models (SSWM) further augment these capabilities by combining slot attention mechanisms with graph neural networks, which capture multi-step dynamics and object interactions in synthetic environments such as Spriteworld ([SSWM](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.03326)). In real-world driving scenarios, SAVi++ introduces depth prediction targets drawn from sparse LiDAR signals into a slot-based video model; this integration enhances tracking performance against challenging dynamic and occlusion-heavy sequences on datasets like Waymo Open ([SAVi++](https://openreview.net/pdf?id=fT9W53lLxNS)). 

Depth signals provide additional, crucial cues that help disentangle static backgrounds from moving objects and ensure robust object segmentation under multi-view conditions. Incorporating these signals reduces the ambiguity inherent in purely RGB-based predictions and improves the network’s ability to maintain object identity across shifts in viewpoint ([Depth Signals and Multi-View Consistency](https://openreview.net/pdf?id=fT9W53lLxNS)). Built-in multi-view fusion modules that dynamically voxelize and aggregate features from different perspectives further enhance cross-view consistency, enabling a reliable reconstruction of object trajectories even in complex environments.

The synthesis of view-invariant objects with robust depth-informed cues represents a significant advancement, though challenges persist. For instance, latent drift over prolonged sequences and performance degradation in occluded scenes demand additional measures such as explicit drift-correction mechanisms and temporal regularization strategies. These methods include exponential moving average smoothing and cross-view consistency penalties that mitigate latent drift and better preserve spatial identities over time.

## Benchmark Suites and Datasets Driving Spatial Reasoning Research
Benchmarking remains a crucial component in evaluating progress in spatial reasoning in world models. Datasets and benchmark suites strive to measure the quality of 3D reasoning, multi-view integration, and temporal dynamics across varied real-world and synthetic environments. Ego3D-Bench, for example, focuses on ego-centric multi-view outdoor scenarios and provides over 8,600 QA pairs demonstrating the gap between human performance and state-of-the-art models. Enhancements through cognitive map augmentation in Ego3D-VLM boost spatial reasoning metrics significantly ([Ego3D-Bench](https://arxiv.org/html/2509.06266v2)). 

OmniSpatial and SPATIALAB further contribute by exploring 3D temporal reasoning and scene-scale spatial interactions, while SPAR-7M/SPAR-Bench combine millions of QA samples and thousands of scenes to test 3D spatial relationships using both single-view and video inputs ([SPAR-7M/SPAR-Bench](https://openreview.net/pdf/6621fdc2d197d605ddbc0aaaea1e93b868df1d37.pdf), [SPATIALAB](https://openreview.net/pdf/fdfd52ad628a84178eac1dac5f689b6398aabdb0.pdf)). 

Unified benchmark suites have come to the forefront, as researchers grapple with mode gaps between single-view, multi-view, and video tasks. CrossVid, 3DSRBench, TAPVid-3D, and OmniGround represent efforts to establish common evaluation protocols that capture the nuances of spatial reasoning across multiple modalities. For instance, 3DSRBench uses specialized evaluations like FlipEval and CircularEval to assess robustness to viewpoint changes, thus highlighting the need for benchmarks that test generalization across diverse perspectives ([3DSRBench](https://www.cs.jhu.edu/~alanlab/Pubs25/ma20253DSRBench.pdf)). 

The integration of multi-modal data (RGB, depth, LiDAR) within these benchmarks is essential for capturing real-world spatial complexity. A unified benchmark suite that standardizes ground-truth annotations for object poses, depths, and trajectories across single, multi-view, and video streams promises to provide direct insights into model strengths and failure modes, thereby guiding further architectural refinements.

## Evaluating Explicit 3D Representations: Point Clouds, Voxels, and Implicit Models
Explicit 3D representations constitute a significant frontier in world modeling. Researchers have compared point clouds, voxels/TSDFs, and hybrid implicit methods to understand how each modality supports planning and spatial reasoning. Models like PointWorld leverage explicit point-centric representations and predict 3D point flow to support dynamic planning, showing promise in zero-shot manipulation tasks on large-scale datasets ([PointWorld](https://openreview.net/pdf/820606bc13c8aca1c4e185ad46e51f278ae71741.pdf)). 

In contrast, GRWM and SAP represent approaches that impose geometry regularization within latent spaces to ensure the fidelity of long-horizon rollouts. GridFormer and GridPull illustrate a hybrid approach where explicit geometry (via point-based or voxel-based representations) is fused with implicit neural features, achieving efficient surface reconstruction and accurate depth estimation ([GridFormer](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.02292), [GridPull](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.13175)). 

Comparative studies indicate that while explicit representations offer sharper geometric fidelity and are more easily interpretable in planning contexts, they often rely on costly sensors and complex pre-processing pipelines. Implicit methods, on the other hand, benefit from scalability and flexibility but may lag in fine-grained planning tasks. Benchmarking these representations on common planning tasks such as SE(3) trajectory optimization in cluttered environments helps isolate their strengths in terms of memory efficiency, speed, and success rate.

A promising research direction advocates for hybrid architectures that combine explicit pointer memories with latent decoders drawn from implicit methods. Such systems could leverage the rich geometric priors from explicit models while maintaining the flexibility required for real-time inference, ultimately meeting the demanding requirements of real-world robotics and AR/VR applications.

## Cross-Dataset Generalization and Robustness Challenges
World models must operate reliably across different spatial layouts, environments, and modalities. Cross-dataset generalization studies reveal that many state-of-the-art models face significant challenges when shifting from indoor to outdoor settings, ego-centric to third-person perspectives, or synthetic to real-world data. Ego3D-Bench, SPAR-7M/SPAR-Bench, SPHERE, and 3DSRBench all expose critical gaps in depth estimation, viewpoint invariance, and temporal consistency that suggest the need for more robust data regimes ([Ego3D-Bench](https://arxiv.org/html/2509.06266v2), [SPAR-7M/SPAR-Bench](https://openreview.net/pdf/6621fdc2d197d605ddbc0aaaea1e93b868df1d37.pdf)).

Models trained primarily on 2D synthetic data combined with 3D-ground-truth-derived signals exhibit strong performance on single-view tasks but often fail to generalize robustly to multi-view or temporally extended scenarios. This discrepancy underscores the importance of systematic ablation studies that compare training signals (2D synthetic vs 3D-ground-truth) across controlled tasks to determine the regimes in which each type of supervision suffices. 

Robustness challenges extend to spatial reasoning under noise, occlusion, and incomplete data. Diffusion-based world models and denoising frameworks have demonstrated that proper sequentialization and conditioning on past observations can dramatically improve performance under degraded input conditions—from under 1% accuracy to over 50% in critical tasks ([Spatial Reasoning with Denoising Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.21075)). Test-time view synthesis strategies, as demonstrated by MindJourney, provide additional resilience by generating alternative viewpoints when occlusion obscures critical scene elements ([MindJourney](https://arxiv.org/pdf/5ed386610c8657ff319e5833b8272c6459dd85a4.pdf)). 

These findings highlight that multi-view conditioning, probabilistic predictions, and sparse latent representations are key enablers of robustness. Yet, achieving high fidelity in object tracking and spatial reasoning under extreme conditions remains an open area for further research, particularly in the context of hybrid training signals and cross-dataset evaluations.

## Memory Fusion Strategies and Temporal Coherence
Long-horizon reasoning and consistent spatial tracking necessitate sophisticated memory fusion strategies. Advanced models combine working memory tokens with episodic memory banks to capture both short-term details and long-range spatial–temporal context. The 3DLLM-MEM approach, for example, utilizes a dynamic memory fusion module that projects 3D features into a fixed token space and selectively retrieves context from an episodic memory store, yielding significant improvements in long-horizon task performance ([3DLLM-MEM](https://openreview.net/pdf/8e3474ff3b680185f73a397352845c1347b77d73.pdf)).

Other strategies, such as those proposed in R³Mem or EM-LLM, structure memory hierarchically at various granularities (document, paragraph, sentence, entity) allowing robust compression and retrieval. These approaches mitigate latent drift—a common issue where object-centric slots lose identity over time—through techniques like exponential moving average (EMA) smoothing and explicit drift-correction losses ([SOLD](https://openreview.net/pdf/96985506cc6e6b51174eaf942ed996801b5fccbd.pdf), [SlotFormer](https://export.arxiv.org/pdf/2210.05861v2.pdf)). 

Empirical work shows that models incorporating cross-view penalties further enforce consistency of object representations when scenes are observed from multiple angles, reducing error propagation in downstream tasks. A hybrid approach that merges working-memory guided episodic fusion with structured external memory (e.g., Memory3) could potentially yield the optimal balance between efficiency and long-term spatial fidelity, especially in applications like robotics navigation where maps and object positions evolve continuously.

The design of a standardized 3D memory benchmark is emerging as a critical adjunct to these advances. By defining task-rich taxonomies that include object memory, spatial layouts, dynamic scene memory, and cross-view fusion, researchers can systematically measure improvements in both accuracy and memory efficiency under realistic conditions ([3DMEM-BENCH](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.15957), [4DWorldBench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.19836)).

## Robustness Under Noise, Occlusion, and Incomplete Data
World models must contend with a myriad of imperfections in real-world input data. In spatial reasoning tasks, noise, occlusion, and incomplete data challenge the reliability of both perception and planning systems. Diffusion World Models (DWM) and robust denoising frameworks illustrate that carefully optimized generation order and conditioning strategies can yield substantial performance improvements; for example, sequentialization can boost accuracy from less than 1% to above 50% on highly challenging tasks ([Spatial Reasoning with Denoising Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.21075)). 

MindJourney further demonstrates that test-time integration—where models synthesize alternative viewpoints in the presence of occlusion—can produce measurable accuracy gains (around 7.7%) without any additional fine-tuning ([MindJourney](https://openreview.net/pdf/5ed386610c8657ff319e5833b8272c6459dd85a4.pdf)). Other approaches incorporate sparse latent representations that are less sensitive to noise and summarize visual scene attributes with greater abstraction, thereby mitigating the impact of occlusion and degradation ([Sparse World Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.11663)).

Innovative conditioning signals, such as incorporating depth, optical flow, or segmentation maps into the latent space (as proposed by Probabilistic Structure Integration, PSI), further enhance robustness by providing additional structure that helps models maintain coherent 3D representations even when faced with partial data ([PSI](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.09737)). Test-time methods, including dynamic ensembling and adaptive sampling strategies (Robust Diffusion Learning, RDC), continue to bolster performance under adverse conditions ([RDC](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.10149)).

The consistent theme is that robust spatial reasoning under real-world degradation benefits from multi-modal inputs, probabilistic prediction, and explicit spatial priors. However, performance gaps remain, particularly for persistent occlusion or extreme noise, signaling a clear need for further innovations in model architecture and training methodologies.

## Unified Benchmark Suite Design for Spatial Reasoning
The establishment of unified, scalable benchmark suites represents a critical pathway for rapid progress in world modeling. Recent efforts aim to integrate diverse evaluation tasks—ranging from single-view and multi-view to video data—under a consistent 3D ground-truth schema. CrossVid, 3DSRBench, TAPVid-3D, Ego3D-Bench, and OmniGround collectively illustrate how benchmarks can capture object tracking, spatial reasoning, depth estimation, and multi-modal fusion in complex environments ([CrossVid](https://arxiv.org/html/2511.12263v1), [3DSRBench](https://www.cs.jhu.edu/~alanlab/Pubs25/ma20253DSRBench.pdf), [Ego3D-Bench](https://arxiv.org/html/2509.06266v2)).

Key design considerations include the standardization of annotation pipelines (with semi-automatic labeling tools and synthetic-to-real transfer approaches), the adoption of shared metrics covering perceptual quality, spatial consistency, and temporal coherence, and the use of human or LLM-based evaluators to align results with real-world spatial understanding. Balancing the breadth of task types with practical annotation burdens remains essential, and proposals for a modular benchmark architecture—comprising a unified 3D ground-truth module, a multi-view consistency layer, and separate evaluation heads for QA, grounding, and tracking—demonstrate a promising route forward ([E3D-Bench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.01933), [4DWorldBench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.19836)).

Unified benchmark suites offer a means not only to quantify model performance but also to diagnose failure modes, compare architectural innovations, and steer research toward closing the gaps in spatial reasoning and cross-dataset generalization.

## Conclusion and Recommendations
Our synthesis of recent progress in world models for spatial reasoning reveals a landscape of significant advances interwoven with persistent challenges. Researchers have advanced techniques for representing 3D geometry and space through explicit modeling, memory-enhanced architectures, and robust depth and multi-view conditioning, yielding impressive gains in both static and dynamic environments. Object-centric architectures equipped with view-invariant latents and slot coherence mechanisms bolster temporal tracking, yet issues such as latent drift and handling of extreme occlusion remain prominent.

Benchmarking efforts such as SPAR-Bench, Ego3D-Bench, and the emerging unified suites offer critical tools for evaluating progress but also reveal a need for standardized, modular evaluation protocols that can comprehensively cover single-view, multi-view, and temporal reasoning tasks. Explicit comparisons of 2D synthetic supervision versus 3D ground-truth–derived training regimes have shown promising trade-offs; however, the boundary at which 3D augmentation becomes essential must be further clarified through systematic ablation studies.

In planning paths forward, we recommend the following:
- Develop standardized, modular benchmark suites that integrate unified ground-truth annotations, multi-view consistency measures, and task-specific evaluation heads to facilitate direct comparisons across diverse world models.
- Invest in hybrid architectures that combine the geometric clarity of explicit 3D representations (point clouds, voxels/TSDF) with the scalability and flexibility of implicit methods, while building geometry-aware evaluation metrics that capture long-horizon rollout fidelity.
- Pursue research on memory fusion strategies and robust per-slot temporal coherence to mitigate latent drift in long-horizon tasks. Incorporate hierarchical and episodic memory designs, EMA-based drift correction, and cross-view penalties to ensure stable object tracking.
- Focus on robust design by integrating depth signals and multi-view conditioning, thereby enhancing resilience to noise, occlusion, and incomplete data. Test-time view synthesis and probabilistic structure integration represent promising strategies in this realm.
- Undertake systematic ablations comparing 2D synthetic and 3D ground-truth training signals across controlled tasks spanning single-view to video to delineate the boundaries where each training regime excels, thereby guiding data collection and model design strategies.

By addressing these recommendations, future world models will become better equipped to navigate the complexities of real-world spatial environments. Researchers and practitioners in robotics, autonomous navigation, AR/VR, and embodied AI will benefit from systems that robustly capture, remember, and reason over spatial data in dynamic, multi-view, and temporally extended scenarios. This synthesis provides a clear blueprint for advancing spatial reasoning in world models, with concrete avenues for closing the gaps between current capabilities and the demands of real-world applications ([SPAR-7M/SPAR-Bench](https://openreview.net/pdf/6621fdc2d197d605ddbc0aaaea1e93b868df1d37.pdf), [SAVi++](https://openreview.net/pdf?id=fT9W53lLxNS), [3DLLM-MEM](https://openreview.net/pdf/8e3474ff3b680185f73a397352845c1347b77d73.pdf)).
